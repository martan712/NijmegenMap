	+ 1. The Human Element: Genealogy & Inhabitants
		The municipal portal tells you where the plots were, but it doesn't easily tell you the drama of who lived there. You can layer in genealogical data to 
		create a "Who Lived Here?" feature.

		The Source: OpenArch.nl or WieWasWie.nl APIs.

		The Data: OpenArch exposes millions of Dutch historical records (birth, marriage, death, and tax registries) via a free API. Many of these records from the
		18th and 19th centuries include exact address fields or old neighborhood codes (like Wijk A, nummer 45).

		The Feature: A user clicks on a building polygon, and your app queries OpenArch for that specific historical address, displaying a timeline of every
		family, merchant, or blacksmith who lived and worked in that building over a 200-year span.

	+ 2. The Architectural Deep Dive: Cultural Heritage API
	The portal has a basic "Historical buildings" layer, but it lacks rich architectural descriptions, interior photos, and restoration histories.

	The Source: Rijksdienst voor het Cultureel Erfgoed (RCE) Open Data & SPARQL Endpoint.

	The Data: The Dutch Cultural Heritage Agency provides full open-access data for every Rijksmonument (national monument) in Nijmegen. This includes the exact
	architectural styles, the names of the original architects, structural modifications over time, and high-resolution cultural asset photos.

	The Feature: Instead of just a popup saying "Built in 1450," your app pulls the official architectural dossier, explaining why the building is culturally
	significant, its Gothic or Renaissance features, and what parts are original versus restored.

	+ 3. Local Lore: The Street Name Origins
	Nijmegen is full of fascinating street names (Kannenmarkt, Ganzenheuvel, Bessemstraat) that directly reflect medieval trades, geography, or historical figures.

	The Source: Regionaal Archief Nijmegen (RAN) Open Data Collections.

	The Data: The regional archive digitizes books and official municipal decrees explaining the exact origin of every street name in the city.

	The Feature: A subtle hover effect or header display. When a user zooms into a street, a small tooltip reveals: "Kannenmarkt: Named in 1432 after the potters and
	pewterers who set up market stalls here to sell jugs (kannen)." It adds instant, bite-sized historical context to the simple act of navigating the map.
	
	+ 4. Media Layer: The Sound of History
	Old maps are silent, but history wasn't. Adding audio or oral histories transforms an app into an emotional experience.

	The Source: Beeld & Geluid (Netherlands Institute for Sound and Vision) open archives, or local walking-tour podcast RSS feeds.

	The Data: Geo-tagged audio clips, radio broadcasts from WWII, and oral history interviews with older Nijmegen citizens recounting life in the mid-20th century.

	The Feature: An "Audio Hotspot" layer. If a user walks near the Waal Bridge with their headphones on, they can trigger an authentic 1944 radio broadcast clipping 	
	detailing the Allied capture of the bridge during Operation Market Garden.
